# Slide Structure

Baseline patterns for building effective presentations. Deck design in `.claude/company/brand.md` overrides visual defaults.

## One Idea Per Slide

If a slide has two ideas, split it. If a slide needs more than 40 words of body text, it is probably a script, not a slide -- put the detail in speaker notes.

## Title Discipline

- Titles are assertions, not labels. "Revenue grew 40% in Q3" beats "Q3 Revenue."
- A reader skimming only the titles should get the argument of the deck.
- Keep titles under 8 words. If you need more, it is two slides.

## Standard Slide Types

### Title slide
- Presentation title
- Subtitle (date, audience, or one-line context)
- Your name / organisation

### Agenda / section divider
- Three to five items maximum
- Label which section is next in mid-deck dividers

### Content slide (standard)
- Title (assertion)
- Body: up to three bullet points OR one visual OR one table OR one quote
- Never all of the above together

### Data slide
- Title states the insight, not the data type ("Conversion rose after redesign" not "Conversion chart")
- Chart occupies the majority of the slide
- Annotation highlights the key data point
- Source line in small text at bottom

### Comparison slide
- Two columns or two rows
- Equal visual weight
- Key difference called out (colour, annotation, arrow)

### Transition slide
- Large single line of text
- Black or brand-colour background
- Use sparingly -- once per section max

### Closing slide
- Single action or next step
- Contact detail
- No thank-you slide unless the audience expects one

## Deck Length Guide

| Format | Target slide count |
|--------|-------------------|
| 15-minute talk | 10-15 slides |
| 30-minute talk | 20-25 slides |
| 45-minute talk | 25-35 slides |
| Workshop (half day) | 30-50 slides plus breakout materials |
| Pitch deck | 10-15 slides |
| Board report | 5-10 slides |

## Structure Templates

### The McKinsey pattern (problem → insight → action)
1. Situation -- what is the context
2. Complication -- what has changed or is at stake
3. Question -- what must we decide
4. Answer -- the recommendation
5. Supporting evidence (3-5 slides)
6. Next steps

### The workshop pattern
1. Hook / opening question
2. Why this matters (context and stakes)
3. The core content (split into 3-5 sections)
4. Practical application / exercise
5. Summary
6. Resources / next steps

### The pitch deck pattern
1. Problem
2. Solution
3. Market
4. Product
5. Traction
6. Business model
7. Team
8. Ask

## Speaker Notes

- Write them. Every slide.
- They are the spoken script -- the slide is the visual aid.
- If you cannot explain the slide without reading the notes, the slide is not doing its job.

## Common Failures

- Text walls no one reads
- Titles that label instead of argue
- Charts without annotations
- Inconsistent fonts, colours, or logo positions
- Apologetic disclaimers on data slides ("data not fully validated")
- Too many builds and animations (each adds friction without adding clarity)
